EICR Failures: Most Common Causes

An Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) is a strict mechanical health check of a property's underlying wiring. When an inspector issues an "Unsatisfactory" outcome grade, it's typically tied to a few recurring code discrepancies that present immediate shock or operational thermal breakdown risks.

Lack of Critical RCD Protection (Code C2)

The single most frequent trigger for an automatic EICR safety fail is the absence of Residual Current Devices (RCDs) on general socket outlets and damp-zone lighting rings. If a circuit lacks a sensitive 30mA trip trigger window, an appliance wiring breakdown could pass live structural currents straight through human contact before standard fuse units register an overload, making this a top-tier safety breach.

Inadequate Main Earthing and Protective Bonding

Testing engineers frequently log degraded, undersized, or missing bonding connections to central oncoming metallic incoming utility infrastructure installations like incoming gas lines and water mains. If a fault causes secondary metal pipes to become live, without correct thick copper connection loops down to the primary electrical earthing rod terminal, the breaker cannot drop fast enough, turning your entire plumbing footprint into an immediate shock risk zone.

Thermal Degradation and DIY Alterations

Overloaded ring main connections often show clear structural signs of heat degradation around terminal points inside wall boxes. Unprofessional layout modifications—like bridging rings, stacking high-draw utilities over loose screw connection lines, or exposing live copper paths inside broken accessories—trigger immediate C1 or C2 failure declarations that require fast isolation remedies.
